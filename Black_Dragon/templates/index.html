What is the DARK WEB???

Information exists at multiple levels of accessibility on the Internet. What most people are familiar with is the ClearNet. Nestled within the Clearnet is the Deep Web. Finally, the Dark Web exists in tandem with the other two, however, inaccessible without the use of special browsers or libraries.

The Clearnet is what most people are familiar with, consisting of websites that are freely available for browsing without needing to authenticate for access. These sites and servers contain unencrypted data, with the exception of HTTPS traffic being encrypted on the wire. An example of this would be a blog site where users do not log in.

The Deep Web can sometimes be conflated with the Clearnet as they're both accessible via normal browsing. This level contains all the data, servers, and web pages that require authentication to access their data. Examples include banking, social media, video streaming, and databases. The only difference between the Deep Web and the Clearnet is the requirement for legitimate credentials. Additionally, some websites (such as YouTube) can provide service in both the Clearnet and Deep Web domains.

The Dark Web does not make the distinction of authenticated/non-authenticated users as it exists in both forms. However, there is a technological difference when compared to the ClearNet and Deep Web. Data in this domain is triple encrypted while in transit and exists at .Onion urls, inaccessible to normal browsers.

What is TOR (The Onion Routing)???

TOR is a tool that provides privacy and anonymity when browsing Internet websites (not restricted to the Dark Web). TOR works by using a layered encryption model to protect against traffic analysis with tools such as WireShark. Additionally, not only is the data protected, but the identities of the sender and receiver (user and destination) are protected as well.

TOR was initially proposed and developed by the Office of Naval Research (ONR) with assistance from DARPA between 1995 - 1997. The TOR Project was funded by multiple sponsors since then. It is currently the 3rd generation of Onion Routing and has been open sourced since 2003.

Fun fact: TOR gets its name from the way its encryption has layers... like an onion or an ogre.

How does TOR work??

Tor is essentially a 3-tiered Virtual Private Network (VPN) tunnel. The 3-tiers consist of Guard, middle, and exit relays. Each relay has its own encryption and can view diffent parts of the packet (data and sender/receiver) but not all of the data. Upon initialization (and other points later on), a Circuit is created, deciding which Guard, relay, and exit relay will be used for communications. TOR Browser creates multiple circuits and destroys/recreates on a regular time basis.

When an .Onion request is made, the Client performs multiple encrptions:
1. The Client encrypts the original data in such a way that only the exit relay can decrypt it.
2. This encrypted data is then encrypted again in such a way that only the middle relay can decrypt it.
3. Finally, this encrypted data is encrypted once more in such a way that only the Guard relay can decrypt it.

The connection is then made to the Guard relay which decrypts its outermost layer, exposing a nested encrypted packet and the address of the middle relay. The Guard relay is the ONLY relay that knows the source of the data. However it does not know the destination nor the contents of the data.

The middle relay receives the forwarded packet and decrypts the middle layer of encryption. It is only able to see an encrypted packet, the Guard relay address, and the exit relay address. Source, destination, and contents are all invisible to the middle relay.

The exit relay receives the final encrypted packet. It then decrypts the final layer of encryption and forwards the packet to the final routing point. It is able to see the final destination and the data contents, however the source of that packet is invisible to a exit relay. If the contents of the packet are unencrypted (i.e. HTTP), the data may be visible in plain text to the Guard relay. It is HIGHLY recommended to visit websites strictly over HTTPS for this reason (as HTTPS encrypts the data in transit).

What's a Hidden Service??

Superfically, it's just a website over TOR! At a more technical level, it's a website that utilizes the TOR proxy in a similar manner as any Client (i.e. Tor Browser). This means that circuits are generated with Guard, Middle, and Exit relays. This also means that the data and privacy protections are provided to the Hidden Service too!

How does a Hidden Service work??

Just like with a Client, a TOR proxy is started, but with additional parameters. These parameters specify the files containing the .onion address and the public/private keys. As a nice feature, MULTIPLE Hidden Services can use the same TOR proxy (simply add their details to the configuration). Additionally, the TOR proxy needs to be configured to receive HTTP and/or HTTPS traffic (HTTPS is preferred and HTTP SHOULD redirect to HTTPS or not be accepted).

When connecting to a TOR Hidden Service, the process is slightly different than connecting to a Clearnet website. With a Clearnet website, the Client (user) generates a circuit for communications. When browsing to a Hidden Service, both the Client AND Hidden Service generate their own circuits. Inside the TOR network, a "rendezvous" point is determined and communications are exchanged there, rather than leaving the TOR network at either end. This allows for continued privacy and anonymity protection for BOTH Client and Hidden Service.
